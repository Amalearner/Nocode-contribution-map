For Organizations

Design Your Contributor Journey

A welcoming contributor journey doesn't happen by accident. Work through each stage below — click the option chips that reflect your project's current or planned approach — then use the builder on the right to capture your decisions and download your journey plan.

1
Discovery — How do people find you?
Before anyone can contribute, they need to know your project exists and feel like someone like them could be involved. What channels bring contributors to your door?
Social media Conference talks Blog posts Word of mouth GitHub trending Open source communities Hackathons University programs Podcast features DEI programs Newsletter features
💡 Tip: Projects that show up in spaces where underrepresented communities already gather attract more diverse contributors.
2
Orientation — Can they understand and orient quickly?
Within minutes of landing on your project, a potential contributor should understand what it does, who it's for, and what kind of help is needed — without having to ask.
Clear README Welcome README CONTRIBUTING.md Contribution types listed Project roadmap Code of Conduct Good first issue labels Non-code tasks labeled Project website Video intro / demo FAQ document
💡 Tip: If your CONTRIBUTING.md only mentions code, it's implicitly excluding everyone else. Name every type of contribution explicitly.
3
First Contribution — What's the easiest meaningful first step?
The first contribution should be low-friction, low-stakes, and genuinely useful. It should be achievable without deep technical knowledge and give the contributor a feeling of real impact.
Fix a typo in docs Improve documentation Translate a page Report a bug Respond to an issue Test a new feature Share the project Write a blog post Add an example Review a pull request Answer a community question
💡 Tip: Label non-code "good first issues" just like code ones. Visibility signals that non-code is a real contribution, not a consolation prize.
4
Retention — How do you keep contributors coming back?
First-time contributors become regulars when they feel connected, valued, and clear on what to do next. What structures help people stay?
Community Slack/Discord GitHub Discussions Regular community calls Office hours Welcome intro call Contributor newsletter Mentorship matching Contributor buddy system Regular check-ins Roadmap transparency Decision-making involvement
💡 Tip: A single intro call or "welcome" message from a maintainer can double contributor retention. The human touch matters enormously.
5
Recognition — How are contributions seen and celebrated?
Recognition closes the loop. Without it, contributors feel invisible and eventually leave. What systems make contributions — especially non-code ones — publicly valued?
All-Contributors bot Named in release notes Contributor spotlight posts Dedicated roles/titles Blog features Speaking opportunities Community awards Public thank-you threads Swag & badges Contribution tracking page Conference sponsorship
💡 Tip: Ask yourself — if a contributor's friends searched their name and your project, what would they find? Recognition should be visible beyond your community.
6
Growth — What paths exist beyond "contributor"?
Sustainable communities need clear growth paths. When contributors can see a future for themselves in a project, they invest more deeply and stay longer.
Maintainer pathway Reviewer role Community manager role Documentation lead Accessibility advocate Event organizer role Governance participation Steering committee Mentoring newer contributors Ambassador program
💡 Tip: Non-code roles need explicit names and responsibilities. "Community Manager" is as real a role as "Core Maintainer." Make it visible.

Recognition Frameworks

How Recognition Works

Recognition is not a nice-to-have — it is infrastructure. When contributions go unseen, contributors leave. These are the proven patterns that make every type of contribution visible, valued, and repeatable.

The recognition principle: What gets seen gets repeated. What gets celebrated gets scaled. Design your recognition systems so that a person who writes documentation, organizes an event, or moderates a forum feels as valued as someone who merges a pull request.
🤖
All-Contributors Specification
A GitHub bot and open spec that adds every contributor to your README — docs, design, translation, talks, accessibility, and more — using emoji-coded contribution types.
  • Works with a simple comment in any PR or issue
  • Generates a visible contributors table in your README
  • Over 30 contribution types recognized out of the box
  • Signals inclusivity to prospective contributors instantly
Add the All-Contributors bot to your repo and define which contribution types matter to you. During onboarding, tell new contributors they will be added after their first contribution — this alone increases follow-through. Review and update the table with each release cycle.
📣
Release Shoutouts
Every release announcement names non-code contributors explicitly. When someone's documentation fix unblocked 300 users, say it publicly.
  • Include a "Community Contributions" section in every changelog
  • Call out specific impact, not just names
  • Post across all project channels — not just GitHub
  • Normalize equal treatment of code and non-code contributions
Create a changelog template that includes a dedicated non-code section. Ask maintainers to tag non-code contributions during the cycle so nothing is forgotten. Even a single sentence — "Thanks to @name for rewriting our onboarding guide" — makes an enormous difference to the person named.
🏷️
Formal Contributor Roles
Create explicit named roles for non-code contributors. These titles are public, meaningful, and give contributors an identity within the project.
  • Community Manager, Documentation Lead, Accessibility Advocate
  • Event Organizer, Outreach Coordinator, Translation Lead
  • List roles in your README and governance documents
  • Assign responsibilities and decision-making authority to each role
Start with one or two roles based on current community needs. Write a short role description: what the person does, what authority they have, and how they can grow from that role. Post it publicly and invite applications or nominations from existing contributors.
🎤
Platform Access & Voice
Invite non-code contributors to speak, write, and represent the project publicly. Giving voice is the most powerful form of recognition.
  • Co-present at conferences with community contributors
  • Feature contributor bylines on the project blog
  • Invite regulars to co-host office hours or community calls
  • Share contributor stories on social media channels
Many contributors have never been asked to speak publicly. A direct invitation — "Would you share what you've built here at our next community call?" — is transformational. Provide speaking coaching and support for those who need it.
🌱
Structured Mentorship Pathways
A mentor's investment is recognition that says "your future here is worth our time." Mentorship turns one-time contributors into long-term community members.
  • Pair new non-code contributors with experienced members
  • Run 30/60/90-day onboarding check-ins
  • Create clear growth milestones for non-code pathways
  • Document mentorship expectations for both sides
A lightweight mentorship program needs just three things: a willing mentor, a 30-minute intro call, and a shared list of first tasks. Consider a buddy system where existing contributors volunteer to guide one newcomer per quarter.
📊
Contribution Tracking & Impact Reporting
Data makes invisible work visible. Tracking non-code contributions creates evidence of their value for contributors, maintainers, and funders alike.
  • Maintain a public contribution log beyond commit history
  • Track events organized, docs improved, issues triaged
  • Include non-code metrics in project health reports
  • Share impact stats with the full community regularly
Build a simple spreadsheet or GitHub project board that tracks non-code contributions quarterly. Share a "Community Impact" summary with every major release: X events hosted, X pages translated, X new contributors welcomed. This normalizes non-code work as part of the project's core output.

⚠️ Anti-Patterns That Drive Contributors Away

These common mistakes actively signal to non-code contributors that they don't fully belong — even when that's not the intention.

Code-only changelogs

Listing only PRs and commits erases documentation, design, community, and accessibility work that shipped alongside them. Every release touch-point should reflect the full team.

No non-code pathways

If someone has to ask "can I help with X?", you have no guide. Silence reads as "non-coders not welcome." Name every contribution type explicitly in writing.

Code of Conduct theater

A CoC that exists but is never enforced is worse than none — it promises safety and then breaks that promise. Assign a real point of contact and enforce it consistently.

Gatekeeping by default

"If you want to help, open a PR" assumes coding fluency. Inclusive projects define multiple doors in, not just one — and make each door clearly visible.

Recognition asymmetry

Thanking code contributors publicly while silently accepting non-code contributions teaches the whole community which work is valued. Symmetry matters.

No growth for non-coders

If the only way to gain trust, authority, or a "maintainer" badge is through code, your community structure excludes most of the people who keep it alive.

For Contributors: This assessment helps you understand your current open source contribution profile — what skills you're already bringing, where you might grow, and what barriers you may be facing. There are no wrong answers. Be honest with yourself; the goal is clarity, not perfection.
A — Skills & Contribution Readiness
How ready do you feel to start or grow your open source contributions right now?
I can clearly name at least three non-code skills I bring to a project (e.g. writing, organizing, designing, translating, researching).
Think broadly — skills from work, volunteering, or personal projects all count.
Not rated
I understand the difference between code and non-code contributions, and I believe both are equally valuable.
Confidence in the value of non-code work matters — imposter syndrome is real in open source.
Not rated
I know which open source projects or communities align with my interests and skill set.
Being a good match for a project makes contribution more sustainable and enjoyable.
Not rated
I am aware of at least two places I could make a first non-code contribution right now (e.g. a project with documentation needs, a translation task, a community question I could answer).

B — Confidence & Barriers
What's holding you back — or what's already cleared out of your way?
I feel confident that I won't be judged or dismissed for contributing without coding skills.
Fear of judgment is one of the top barriers for non-code contributors. Be honest about where you are.
Not rated
I understand what tools or platforms are typically used in open source communities (GitHub, Discord, Slack, Figma, etc.) well enough to get started.
Technical tooling can feel like a barrier — even for non-code contributors.
Not rated

Visualizing growth makes it more likely to happen.
Not rated
I know how I would like to be recognized for my contributions — and I'm comfortable asking for that recognition if it doesn't happen automatically.
Knowing your recognition preferences helps you find communities that match your needs.
Not rated
I feel like I belong in open source — that it is a space for people like me, with my background and skills.

For Projects: This assessment helps maintainers and community managers evaluate how inclusive, welcoming, and structured their project is for non-code contributors. Rate honestly — the gaps you identify are your highest-value improvement opportunities.
A — Discoverability & First Impressions
Can potential non-code contributors find your project and immediately understand they're welcome?
Our project is actively promoted in spaces where non-technical communities gather (social media, events, newsletters, DEI spaces).
Discoverability for non-coders requires presence in places they already are.
Not rated
Our README explicitly welcomes non-code contributors and describes at least three ways they can contribute that don't involve writing code.
The README is often the first and last thing a potential contributor sees. It sets the tone entirely.
Not rated
We use labels like "good first issue (non-code)" or equivalent to make non-code entry points as visible as code entry points.
Visibility of non-code tasks in the issue tracker is one of the highest-impact, lowest-effort changes a project can make.
Not rated
B — Onboarding & Orientation Quality
When a non-code contributor arrives, how clearly and quickly can they figure out how to help?
Our CONTRIBUTING.md includes specific guidance for non-code contributors — not just developers.
A CONTRIBUTING.md that only describes how to submit code PRs tells non-coders they don't belong here.
Not rated
A first-time contributor can make a meaningful non-code contribution within one hour of discovering our project, without needing to ask for help.
This is the "1-hour test." If your answer is no, your onboarding has friction that is losing contributors.
Not rated
We have a functional, welcoming community channel (Slack, Discord, GitHub Discussions) where new contributors can introduce themselves and get a warm response.
A channel that exists but isn't actively tended is worse than none — it creates the feeling of walking into an empty room.
Not rated
C — Recognition, Retention & Community Safety
Do contributors feel seen, safe, and motivated to stay?

Asymmetric recognition teaches the community which work matters. Symmetry is essential.
Not rated

Named roles are signals that non-code contributors have a real and permanent place in the project structure.
Not rated
Our Code of Conduct is actively enforced, with a named contact person and a clear, trusted process for reporting issues.
A CoC without enforcement is the most common form of false safety signaling in open source communities.
Not rated
We have clear growth pathways for non-code contributors — they can see how to move from first contribution to a named role or leadership position over time.
Without visible growth paths, contributors plateau and leave. Defining ladders retains people and builds leadership depth.
